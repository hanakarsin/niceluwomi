Natembeya Orders Chiefs to be Mcees in Burial Services to Ensure COVID-19 Rules Are Followed

Posted by Lourie Helzer on Tuesday, February 20, 2024

- George Natembeya said the move was to ensure that the ceremonies were performed in the shortest time possible

- The chiefs will also be required to restrict the number of speakers and mourners at burial ceremonies to 100

- They are also expected to ensure that no drinking or feasting takes place

Rift Valley regional commissioner George Natembeya has instructed all chiefs to act as masters of ceremonies during burial services.

This partly is to ensure that the ceremonies are performed in the shortest time possible.

Speaking to the press on Friday, March 19, Natembeya further instructed chiefs to restrict the number of speakers and mourners at burial ceremonies in their jurisdictions to 100.

They are also expected to ensure that no drinking or feasting takes place.

“The COVID-19 positivity rate that we were all happy and very excited about when it dropped to 4% has now shot up to an incredible 14% in the past week," said Natembeya.

"This is almost four times what it was before," he added.

The regional commissioner said the reality was reflected in increased diagnosis and hospitalisation of patients in need of critical care.

"The window of intervention is small and dependent on the timing,” he warned.

The administrator announced the creation of a multi-agency team to implement containment measures aimed at slowing down the spread of the coronavirus while releasing the COVID-19 situation report at the regional offices in Nakuru.

The National Police Service, Kenya Prison Service, Directorate of Criminal Investigations, National Security Intelligence Service, and the National Transport Safety Authority will form the Multi-agency Compliance Unit (NTSA).

Natembeya also instructed police chiefs that no public or private vehicles should be permitted on the roads after 10pm.

Natembeya stated that he was aware that virtually all PSVs operating within estates and major highways in the area had disobeyed a national government order issued by the Health Cabinet Secretary to reduce the number of passengers they transport.

Most Public Service Vehicles (PSVs), according to Natembeya, were not providing sanitisers, soap, or water to their passengers as needed.

He warned the public not to board any vehicles transporting passengers in excess of the health minister's directive.

“We will arrest transporters who are yet to comply with the advisory," he said."All Kenyans have an individual responsibility to do their part because if the virus is not contained it may hurt the economy," added Natembeya.

The Regional Commissioner bemoaned the fact that politicians were the weakest link in the virus's spread.

He directed police to ensure that meetings, including those in private homes, are not convened or licensed.

Natembeya said public health officers were under instructions to crack the whip on supermarkets, hotels and restaurants that were not observing guidelines and regulations aimed at controlling the spread of the virus.
