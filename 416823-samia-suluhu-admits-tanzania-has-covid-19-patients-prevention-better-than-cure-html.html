Tanzania's President Samia Suluhu Hassan has admitted that East Africa's populous nation has COVID-19 patients.

Since taking office in March 2021, Suhulu has shown commitment to handling the pandemic despite assertions that the country had trounced over the virus.

Suluhu addressed the Bishops of the Tanzania Episcopal Conference (TEC) at their headquarters in Kurasini, Dar es Salaam on Friday, June 25, pleaded with members of the clergy to pray for the COVID-19 patients.

Members of clergy intervention

She also urged the religious leaders to tell believers to take precautions.

The head of state who did not reveal any data on COVID-19 patients maintained that it is important for everyone to take prevention since it is better than cure.

''It is important for people to continue to use whatever is prescribed to prevent the epidemic from preventing the nation from numerous deaths,'' said Suluhu.

This is the first time that the government of Tanzania has come out to report the prevalence of the pandemic in the country since May 2020.

The country is currently battling the Third Wave of the pandemic, with Suluhu calling on the clergy to be at the forefront in spearheading the fight against the virus.

“It is clear that from the beginning, the church has had a strong position in the fight against the coronavirus disease. However, we have entered the third wave of the disease, and signs in the country are visible; we already have patients who have been diagnosed in this wave," she revealed.

Suluhu observed that she found patients with such problems during her recent visit to Mwananyamala Hospital in Dar es Salaam.

"It is now evident that this matter is here with us, and we must not give up in this fight. Therefore, I ask you, the religious leaders, to speak out loud to your believers to avoid mass deaths in the country. We should also ask God to spare us from this tragedy,” said Suluhu.

COVID-19 evaluation committee

On May 17, Suluhu received a report from the COVID-19 evaluation committee, which she had formed to assess the virus situation in the country before issuing advice.

The report indicated that Tanzania had experienced two big waves of the pandemic since March 2020, when the first cases were reported.

The committee advised the president to authorise state agencies to conduct free vaccination meeting World Health Organisation (WHO) standards.
