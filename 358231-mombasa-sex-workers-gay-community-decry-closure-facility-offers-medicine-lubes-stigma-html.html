Mombasa gay community decries closure of facility that offers them medicine, lubes without stigma

Posted by Lourie Helzer on Tuesday, June 4, 2024

- The members of the LGBTQ community urged the government to consider HIV as a crucial disease and reopen the facility to serve them

- They argued business had to go on as usual since they could not visit another facility to get the essential drugs due to stigmatisation

- The over 500 LGBTQ added the government's focus was now only on the COVID-19 pandemic, neglecting patients suffering from other diseases like HIV and chronic diseases

Hundreds of sex workers and members of the LGBTQ community have suffered a blow following closure of Kenya Medical Research Institute (KEMRI) where they usually get treatment and lubricants.

James Mugambi who is a gay volunteer at the KEMRI said the closure of the institution which he visited on a monthly basis to collect antiretroviral (ARV's) had hit him hard.

Mugambi told TUKO.co.ke that since 2014, he had been interacting with the doctors from the centre.

“I am more comfortable dealing with medics at the facility because they understand me and I am never stigmatized. But in public health facilities, stigma is rife."I pick my dose of ARV's, lubricants and condoms from the centre, but after its closure, I was referred to a different one which I feel so difficult to express myself,” he said.

Mugambi urged the government to consider the Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV) as a crucial disease and reopen the facility to serve the gay community.

Another male sex worker, Shakira, said he had been receiving Pre-exposure prophylaxis (or PrEP) from the centre for the past 11 years and its closure meant death for him.

He says that for the past two months, he has not been able to access the crucial drug adding that he is endangering his life.

“I have no option but to keep on having sex with other men since I need to survive. However, the abrupt cut of PrEP has put me in danger and risking my clients lives,” he said.

He argued that business had to go on as usual since he could not visit another facility to get the essential drugs due to stigmatization.

“I can't let go of my KSh 10, 000 profit business since I am the breadwinner of my family of five,” he added.

The LGBTI community living in North Coast, are now up in arms over the closure of the centre.

CK Sugar, a sex worker, said life had become a living hell after the facility became out of bound for them.

“Visiting the facility when unwell makes me feel so great. The service providers are so welcoming. But for now, I will have to seek alternative ways of getting medication,” she said.

The over 500 LGBTQ raised concerns saying that the government's focus was now on the COVID-19 pandemic while patients suffering from other diseases like HIV had been neglected.

Bishop Lawrence Chai of Free Apostolic Churches of Kenya has strongly condemned the move by the government to close down the Mtwapa research centre saying it was ill-advised.

“The Mtwapa research centre is a clinic as any other and most people especially those living with HIV access services from it. Closing it was unnecessary," he said.

"It is so sad knowing that the centre had been closed because of COVID-19, putting in mind that HIV cases are going up every day and people are still having sex,” he added.

Chai further added that the centre is of great help not only to Mtwapa people but other people coming from Likoni area in Mombasa County, Kaloleni and Chonyi.

The clergy argued that COVID-19 and HIV had no difference since no cure had been identified for both viruses.

“As a committee member in the centre, I was shocked to visit and find it closed down I just saw a notice pinned on its doors," he said."We, therefore, ask the government to reconsider and reopen it up as soon as possible,” he added.

But HAPA Kenya, an organization that deals with key population told TUKO.co,ke it had received numerous number of members of the LGBTQ in need of their services.

When contacted, KEMRI director, Benjamin Tsofa said the institute had scaled down its operations to other activities and concentrate more on the fight against COVID-19.

“A circular was produced by the government for all KEMRI facilities to scale down their activities and focus on COVID-19 testing," he saidWe had to move non-essential services to other county facilities in a bid to stop the spread of the virus,” he added.
