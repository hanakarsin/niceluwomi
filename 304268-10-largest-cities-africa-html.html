10 largest cities in Africa

Posted by Enda Jantzen on Monday, May 20, 2024

Urbanisation is taking Africa by a storm, and rural to urban migration is now more emphasised than ever before. The populations in urban areas, especially cities, are continuously burgeoning to form the largest cities in Africa, while the once small towns are elevating their status to huge cities. One major characteristic of all cities and more so, the top cities in Africa is the challenge of overcrowding, as the rate of expansion can hardly accommodate the growing population.

The rapid increase in Africa’s cities in terms of their population is mainly catalysed by the need for a better life and the pursuit for greener pastures. As such, the increase presents both opportunities and challenges in nearly equal measure. Positively, population increase enhances the rapid growth of the now biggest cities in Africa through the availability of affordable labour and market for products. Negatively, the initially available resources are getting overstrained to accommodate the ballooning population, with tendencies of some people resorting to slums and street life.

Additionally, there is a continuous increase in the cost of living with products in urban centres getting more expensive, trying to balance the supply-demand curves. Most cities are however seeking measures and opportunities to not only respond to the changing population landscapes through devising novel ideas on food supply but also creating new housing mechanisms to ensure all city residents are appropriately housed and can comfortably access basic needs.

Largest cities in Africa by population

As aforementioned, the rate of growth accrues both benefits and challenges. However, cities which have responded positively to the challenge of overpopulation are now reaping the benefits presented by the rise. As such, Africa hosts some of the fastest growing cities both in the continent and in the world. Here is a list of ten top cities in Africa to get you started.

1. Lagos

Country: Nigeria | Population: 21 million

Lagos is one of the biggest cities in Africa, both by population and urban agglomeration. Also, it is ranked as one of the world’s fastest growing cities currently at 7th position worldwide by the Heinrich-Boll-Stiftung (HBS) Foundation research. It is one of the major economic hubs of Africa with the largest GDP.

The economy of the city expands to transport, manufacturing, service, construction retail, and commercial sectors. Further, Lagos megacity houses one of the continent’s largest and busiest seaports and the largest employer of Lagos residents. It originated from what was originally a port made up of several islands along the Atlantic Ocean, to one major city. Speedy populace led to Lagos extending to the mainland classifying the city into both island and mainland.

2. Cairo

Country: Egypt | Population: 19.5 million

Located in the Nile Delta, Cairo is undoubtedly one of the fastest growing cities in Africa. In Egypt, it serves as the largest city and also the capital town. Further, the metropolitan has the largest urban area in Africa and the Middle East. After Jakarta and Karachi, Cairo’s urban area is also listed as the third largest Muslim town in the world. Economically, two-thirds of the country’s GDP is generated by the city and consequently serving as Egypt’s financial hub, with its commercial activities diversifying to textile, food processing, consumer good production, iron and steel manufacturing, tourism, trade, and finance.

3. Kinshasa

Country: Democratic Republic of Congo (DRC) | Population: 11.8 million

Ever wondered which country has the 3rd largest city in Africa? Here it is, the capital city for the Democratic Republic of Congo. Positioned alongside Congo River the second longest river in Africa after the Nile, Kinshasa can now be termed as a megacity that revolutionised from fishing sites and trading villages. The phenomenon about this city is its closeness to Brazzaville the capital city of the bordering Republic of the Congo. It makes it unique same as Rome and Vatican City in Europe and hence the second most close pair of capital cities in the world.

4. Johannesburg

Country: South Africa | Population: 7.86 million

After the discovery of Gold from what was initially a farm in 1886, Johannesburg has now grown to be one of the largest cities in Africa and also South Africa’s major city. Further, it serves as the capital city of Gauteng province, the wealthiest province in the country. The city sits on large scale deposits of gold and diamond. The metropolitan is now considered a global city after it was listed by the Globalization and World Cities Research Network.

Its population density is said to be moderate but grew more when the settlements of Soweto (South-Western Townships) was incorporated into the city in 1994. Soweto had natively been inhabited by instinctive African gold mine workers, who were not permitted to live in Johannesburg or mix with the white populace as per the South African policy on segregation also known as apartheid. The conurbation is a continental economic hub for technology, mining, manufacturing, retail and other services including hospitality.

5. Luanda

Country: Angola | Population: 7.8 million

Luanda is one of the top cities in Africa that serves as the nation’s administrative centre and also busiest main harbour to the Atlantic Ocean. The port is country’s main door to the export of petroleum, diamonds, fish products, coffee cotton, sugar, salt, and iron ore. Also, bulk import of steel, iron, machinery, flour, and coal enter the country through Luanda’s port. After Angola’s colonisation by Portugal with São Paulo da Assunção de Loanda, it was the most heavily populated Portuguese-speaking city in Africa.

6. Casablanca

Country: Morocco | Population: 6.86 million

Casablanca is a city located in the western part of Morocco and borders the Atlantic Ocean. Even though the country’s political capital is Rabat, Casablanca is one of the biggest cities in Africa and is considered to be the country’s economic hub. The Royal Moroccan Navy is based in this city with also Port of Casablanca, being one of the largest artificial ports in the world and the second largest port in North Africa, handling approximately 21.3million tons of cargo annually. The megacity attracts 32 percent and 56 percent of production units and industrial labour in the Moroccan economy respectively.

7. Nairobi

Country: Kenya | Population: 6.54 million

Traditionally referred to as the green city under the sun, Nairobi is the largest city and capital of Kenya. The name Nairobi is derived from a Maasai name ‘Enkare Nairobi’ that is translated as cool waters derived from Nairobi river that flows across the city. This metropolitan contributes up to 60 per cent of the country’s GDP and serves as a financial hub to East and Central Africa, thanks to its dynamic technology, commerce, manufacturing, industries, and tourism. The city also hosts the Nairobi National Park, making it the only city bordered by a game reserve in the world.

8. Khartoum

Country: Sudan | Population: 5.27 million

Khartoum is the executive city of Sudan, the continent’s third largest country in the geographical area. It is located at the convergence of the White Nile from Lake Victoria and the Blue Nile flowing from Ethiopia. The city is linked to Khartoum and Omdurman by bridges to form the Khartoum conurbation. The city sprang from an Egyptian army camp to later grow into a military base army town.

The Megalopolis conspicuous infrastructure includes Sudan National Museum, the palace, national parliament and the University of Khartoum. The city is also a major producer of gums, textile, and glass. Further, it hosts Sudan’s largest oil refinery, creating jobs and providing oil for the population.

9. Alexandria

Country: Egypt | Population: 5.2 million

Alexandria city was founded by Alexandria the Great in 331 BC. He individually designed the urban centre by integrating the best of Hellenic arrangement and architecture. The Metropolitan is Egypt’s second largest city that serves as a Mediterranean port and a beach city popular for its tourism destinations. Still standing in the city is a prehistoric monument of the Roman triumphal column, the Pompey’s Pillar in the hill near city Arab Cemetery.

Alexandria is vital to the country’s economy as an industrial and commercial centre with warehousing, manufacturing, banking, shipping, food processing, cement, and petrochemicals produced in the metropolis. It houses four ports namely the western port that handles 60percent of Egypt’s imports and exports (major source of employment), Eastern port - the cruisers harbour, Dekhela port, and Abu Qir Port for commercials.

10. Abidjan

Country: Ivory Coast | Population: 4.7 million

Located to the south-east coast of the Ivory Coast, Abidjan is the country’s capital as well as one of the biggest cities in Africa. With France as the country’s colonial master, Abidjan was assimilated to be one of the most populated French-speaking cities in the globe. The city acts as the country’s urbanisation and industrialisation hub, with Abidjan port serving as the channel to export of cocoa, sawmill, pineapple, bananas, and manganese.

Remarkable tourist attraction locations include Hôtel Ivoire the West African only ice rink, cinema, bowling alley and casino and iconic St. Paul’s Cathedral. Also, Banco National Park and tropical rainforest are magnificent sceneries for tourists to the city. Northern and southern Abidjan is conjoined by the ancient Henri Konan Bédié and the Charles de Gaulle bridges. Abidjan’s population is expected to rise further in the coming years as the rate of development keeps soaring.

Africa is indisputably rising. Cities in this continent have shown upward growth with even adoption of modern infrastructure compared to the medieval times. Urbanisation over the years has majorly led to the growth and expansion of most cities, with masses moving to the urban centres in search of a better life. The largest cities in Africa are faced with various challenges, owing to the imbalance between population growth and the rate of development. Rising rates of insecurity, growth of slums, poor health and sanitation, and environmental degradation are one of the major issues these top cities of Africa have to face.
